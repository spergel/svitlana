Fractal dimension: log(2) / log(piece scale)
Classic (remove 1/3): dim ≈ 0.631.

What are you seeing?

Start with a line. Remove the middle third. You have two shorter lines. Remove the middle third of each of those. Repeat — forever. What you are left with is the Cantor set: infinitely many points, yet with total length zero. It is more than a finite collection of dots (it contains uncountably many points), but “thinner” than any interval. Its fractal dimension — around 0.63 for the classic 1/3-removal case — is a precise way of measuring how much “stuff” is left.

Why does the 2D view look like scattered dots at high iterations? At 7 iterations, there are 27 = 128 tiny intervals in each direction, giving 128×128 = 16,384 squares, each occupying less than 0.1% of the area. The set is genuinely becoming “dust”, and the screen just cannot show how fine it is. Try 3–4 iterations to see the structure clearly; use the Animate button to watch it appear step by step.

Why does this matter for mathematics? Elliptic equations (like heat flow or electrostatics) are well understood when the boundary of the domain is a smooth curve or surface. But what if the boundary is a fractal — or a Cantor-like set with holes at every scale? Guy David, Joseph Feneuil, and Svitlana Mayboroda built the entire theory of harmonic measure and Green functions for exactly that situation. See: David, Feneuil & Mayboroda, Astérisque 2023.
