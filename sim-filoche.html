What are you seeing?

This is the localization landscape u(x,y) — the solution to (−Δ + V)u = 1 — rendered as a 3D surface. The peaks (red tips) are where u is largest, which means W = 1/u is smallest: these are the wells where quantum states concentrate.

In plain terms: imagine the floor is covered in hills and valleys shaped by the disorder. A ball dropped anywhere will roll into one of the valleys and stay there. Quantum particles do the same — they get trapped in these wells and cannot escape, even though they are waves that normally spread freely. This is Anderson localization.

The red lines on the floor are the watershed partition: they divide the domain into regions, each owned by one peak. Every quantum state “belongs to” the nearest peak. This is the key result of Filoche & Mayboroda, PNAS 2012.
